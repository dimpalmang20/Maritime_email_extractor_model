The European Regulatory Trifecta: FuelEU, ETS, and REDiii
The European Union has  three major regulations that will come into full effect or see significant updates in the coming year. These measures are allegedly designed to accelerate the adoption of cleaner fuels and reduce the sector's greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions and absolutely not about raising revenue….. (apparently) regardless of our feelings, the efficacy and the efficiency of spend, these regulations are here to be complied with.
FuelEU Maritime: Driving GHG Intensity Reduction
From January 1, 2025, the FuelEU Maritime regulation will be fully applicable to all ships of 5,000 gross tonnage (GT) and above calling at EU and European Economic Area (EEA) ports . The regulation mandates a gradual reduction in the GHG intensity of the energy used on board, starting with a 2% reduction in 2025 and progressively increasing to an 80% reduction by 2050. This initiative is designed to stimulate demand for renewable and low-carbon fuels, fundamentally altering the fuel mix in the long term.
EU ETS: The Rising Cost of Carbon
The EU Emissions Trading System (ETS),  will see its requirements intensify. In 2025, shipping companies will be required to surrender carbon allowances for 70% of their verified emissions, up from 40% in the introductory year. This will escalate to 100% of emissions in 2026 . The scheme covers 100% of emissions for voyages within the EU and 50% for voyages starting or ending at an EU port. The increasing carbon price will directly translate to higher operational costs for vessels trading in Europe, making fuel efficiency and lower-carbon fuel choices more critical than ever.
REDiii and the Rise of Regulation Arbitrage
Looking ahead to January 1, 2026, the implementation of the Renewable Energy Directive (REDiii) in the Netherlands will bring another layer of complexity and cost to the Amsterdam-Rotterdam-Antwerp (ARA) bunkering hub . Bunker supplies in Dutch ports will be subject to the directive's sustainability and GHG emissions-saving criteria, with compliance costs expected to be passed on to customers as premiums. This development is likely to create opportunities for regulation arbitrage, where ship operators may find more competitive bunker prices at ports outside the immediate jurisdiction of REDiii, potentially shifting some demand away from Rotterdam to other regional ports.
 
Global Operational Changes: MFM, SOLAS, and Argentina
Beyond the EU's ambitious climate package, several other operational and regional regulations will come into force, impacting transparency, safety, and bunkering logistics.

Mass Flow Meters (MFM)
Jan 1, 2026
Mandatory use of MFM systems for all bunker deliveries in the ports of Rotterdam and Antwerp-Bruges. This will enhance transparency and accuracy in bunkering operations, reducing quantity disputes .

SOLAS Flash Point
Jan 1, 2026
Amendments to SOLAS will require the flash point of the fuel to be recorded on the Bunker Delivery Note (BDN). This enhances safety by ensuring that fuel supplied to ships complies with the minimum 60°C flash point requirement .

Argentina Customs Rules
Jan 1, 2026
Argentina will lift restrictions on bunker fuel quantities, allowing vessels to lift fuel up to their full tank capacity. This change removes a significant logistical hurdle and is expected to make Argentina a more attractive and competitive bunkering location .
 
Market Outlook and Key Takeaways
The regulatory wave of 2025-2026 presents both challenges and opportunities for the bunker industry. The increased cost of compliance within the EU will necessitate careful voyage planning and fuel procurement strategies. The potential for regulation arbitrage could lead to shifts in traditional bunkering patterns, benefiting ports that offer a cost advantage.
