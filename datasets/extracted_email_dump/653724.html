We have opened moderately down this morning. 

On Wednesday, oil prices had remained range bound despite the rising geopolitical tensions and the anticipated US Federal Reserve cut, as the focus continues to be largely placed on the looming global glut. The Fed had announced that is has decided to lower the target for its key lending rate by 0.25 percentage points, which will put the lending rate in a range of 3.50 to 3.75 percent. This seemed to have little, to no, impact on oil traders, likely due to the fact that the bank had suggested that there\'d be only one more cut next year, unless there\'s new economic data to dictate further changes. Furthermore, Brent crude had settled up by a tepid 27 cents to $62.21 per barrel, and West Texas Intermediate had settled up by 21 cents to $58.46 after the US had seized a VLCC oil tanker off the coast of Venezuela, boosting fears that the US is drawing closer to conduct a regime change-motivated attack on Venezuela. It should also be mentioned that Ukraine had attacked a Russian shadow-fleet tanker, making it at least the fifth attack in December on vessels that has ties to Russia, despite the ongoing peace talks between Russia and Ukraine. If attacks continue, it will potentially jeopardise the alleged progress in the peace deal talks and could lead to an increase in geopolitical tensions. "The US seizing a sanctioned vessel off Venezuela, and Ukraine attacking another Russian shadow-fleet vessel in the Black Sea should be adding more to the near-term sanction and war-risk premium... The developing super-glut will weigh on prices in 2026", stated Robert Rennie, head of commodity and carbon research at Westpac Banking Corp. The volatility and uncertainty from geopolitical tensions and warnings of a global glut has been weighing heavily on market sentiment, putting pressure on prices.

Crude oil prices had jumped slightly higher on Wednesday after the US had seized a sanctioned tanker off of Venezuela\'s coast, which the Venezuelan government has denounced the seizure, calling it “a blatant theft” and an act of “international piracy.” This had boosted fears of disruptions if the US further escalates the tensions between the two nations. However, prices has dipped again today as the focus is shifted back to oversupply concerns amid weakening demand. Citi has said that it expects Brent crude will drop further to an average of $60 per barrel throughout the first quarter of 2026, and that inventories will keep increasing in the OECD, reported Reuters citing the bank\'s bearish case for oil. For the entirety of next year, Citi\'s base case for the international benchmark is an average price of $62, but the bank\'s bullish case is that Brent crude will see rises to average $75 per barrel over next year, “with realized geopolitical supply disruptions”. The bank\'s full-2026 bearish scenario forecasts that Brent crude will be averaging $50 over the course of next year, based on “geopolitical deal making, less China buying, [and] more OPEC+ supply ahead of the U.S. midterms.” Notably, Citi analysts have said that OPEC+ will potentially extend the pause on production cut rollbacks in order to support prices amid the projected supply overhang next year, which will possibly even extent into 2027. This forecast follows closely after a Bloomberg report citing analysts who expects that China will continue purchasing elevated rates of oil for stockpiling, which would "mask" weakening global demand. ING commodity analysts, Warren Patterson and Ewa Manthey, have warned that “The oil market is moving deeper into the expected oil glut", adding that the pressure it is posing on prices will increase in 2026. “However, Russian oil supply remains a risk. While Russian seaborne export volumes are holding up well, these barrels are struggling to find buyers,” the analysts further noted, mentioning that Urals would need to become cheaper in order to find buyers.

Unsurprisingly, crude oil exports from non-sanctioned companies have jumped since the 21st of November, after the latest US sanctions had been implemented on Rosneft and Lukoil. The two oil majors had been responsible for approximately half of Russia\'s total oil exports, at around 2 million barrels daily, until the sanctions came into effect, which had led importers and exports to seek ways around the sanctions. Since the US sanctions had come into effect, international oil flows from Rosneft and Lukoil had dropped by roughly 1 million barrels daily, according to recent data quoted by Goldman Sachs. However, oil flows from non-sanctioned Russian oil companies to international clients have gained half a million barrels daily during that same period. Currently, Indian refiners are set to buy the most Russian crude in six months this December, regardless of the sanctions and the warnings from the US to discontinue purchases of Russian energy commodities. India is estimated to import a daily average of 1.85 million barrels, up from 1.83 million barrels daily in November, according to data from Kpler, cited by Reuters’ Clyde Russell; this is the highest rate since June, when Indian buyers had imported  2.1 million barrels daily of Russian crude oil. To note, November\'s daily average of 1.83 million barrels is comparably higher to October\'s figure, that stood at 1.48 million barrels daily, per Kpler data. The November figure is a downward revision from earlier estimates, which the month\'s average daily import rate had been forecasted at 1.855 million barrels. Some analysts had forecasted that Indian purchases of Russian oil would significantly drop after the sanctions came into effect, especially since the US has been historically harsh on India for its continued purchases of Russian energy commodities. Yet, recent data has disproved these predictions and has confirmed the expectations that oil flows would continue even with Lukoil and Rosneft out of the picture for now.
