We have opened flat following by moderate rises yesterday.

Crude oil prices are trending down today, set to hit a second consecutive weekly loss as worries of a global glut grows larger amid reports of rising oil inventories. All the major forecasters for the oil market expects that there will be an oversupply around the world in 2026 and that prices will drop even lower; some have noted that Brent could slip near $50 per barrel. Meanwhile, the amount of crude oil stored on water has reached 1.3 billion, which would be the largest amount of crude on water since March 2020, commented Reuters’s Ron Bousso earlier today cited Kpler figures. Kpler\'s data had also showed that the volume of oil stored on tankers is also rising and that for at least 20 days, the amount of crude oil in floating storage had reached 51 million barrels, adding to the already-deepening concerns of an oversupply. However, we did see oil prices spike earlier this week after Trump had announced that he is tightening the “tanker blockade” pressure on Venezuela, which had already been disrupting Venezuela\'s crude oil flows to buyers. In addition to this, it was reported that the UK has imposed sanctions on Tatneft, Russneft, and NNK in a move to step up its pressure on Russia, since the three smaller oil producers had been “obtaining a benefit from or supporting the government of Russia". The additional sanctions, that follows shortly after the latest US sanctions on Rosneft and Lukoil, comes as crude oil flows from the two largest Russian exporters are experiencing a decline in its oil flows. This has provided an opportunity for smaller exporters to ramp up their international shipments, which buyers such as India and China - who have been largely influenced by the effects of the US sanctions - are likely to get their supply from the smaller refineries until they find a way around the sanctions.

On the 14th of December, a Ukrainian drone strike had damaged an offshore drilling rig at Lukoil’s Grayfer field in the Russian Caspian Sea, causing operations to halt at all 14 wells at the platform, said the Ukrainian General Staff earlier this week. Grayfer, on top of the Filanovsky and Korchagin oil fields in the north Caspian Sea, which are operated by Lukoil, have all been hit by drones in the past week after Ukraine had escalated its campaign against Russia\'s energy infrastructure last week, despite the ongoing peace deal talks. Lukoil had discovered the Filanovsky oil field in 2005, and eventually brought it online in 2016; it is considered as the largest oil discovery in decades within Russia, its reserves estimated at 129 million tons of oil and 30 billion cubic meters of natural gas. The Filanovsky field had been attacked on the 11th of December, forcing the offshore platform to suspend its oil and gas production from over 20 wells that\'s servicing the facility. Additionally, Ukraine’s security services had executed a long-range drone strike on a major offshore oil field in the Caspian Sea, which had effectively halted production at a key Lukoil facility. The Caspian Sea is a vital line for global energy markets, the region serving as a crucial hub for Russian output and Central Asian exports for energy commodities. This means that the long-range attacks on these offshore platforms could have a relatively large impact on trading that relies on the Caspian Sea for transporting cargo. Ukraine has been intensifying its attacks on Russia’s oil refineries, depots, and export terminals over the past few weeks, meanwhile, it has been noticed that Russia has boosted its focus on Ukraine’s gas producing facilities and gas and power distribution networks as winter reached its peak and temperatures drop. Considering the fact that the attacks have escalated amid peace deal talks between the two nations, it is likely that the attacks will persist despite the renewed efforts to broker a peace deal from the US. The peace deal negotiations has put pressure on the markets over the past few weeks as many traders and analysts have concerns that a peace deal would lead to the sanctions on Russian energy to be lifted, allowing for Russian oil to flood the market during an expected global glut in 2026. However, if tensions between Russia and Ukraine persists, it could extend - and potentially add - to the volatility in the markets, the consequent uncertainty likely to weigh on prices further.

The US and Ukraine had both signalled earlier this week that there has been progress in the peace deal negotiations during talks in Berlin. It has been reported that the United States has now offered security guarantees to Ukraine that are modelled on NATO’s Article 5 mutual defence pledge. Even though it appears that Trump wants to broker the deal by Christmas,  finalised agreement doesn’t currently seem likely since the key issues regarding territory, is still unresolved; Zelenskyy has made it clear that Ukraine isn’t willing to give up its reclaimed land, whilst Putin has signalled that he wants all of the occupied territory. Zelenskyy had commented on Tuesday that a draft of the US-brokered deal could be presented to Russia within days, but it still remains uncertainty on how Russia would react since every draft made so far has been rejected, insisting that Russia\'s own territorial claims and conditions must be met. Due to Russia\'s the lack of compromise and its unwillingness to seriously participate during past ceasefire negotiations, the US is now considering to escalate the sanctions against Russia\'s oil exports if Putin rejects the peace agreement this time, said anonymous sources with knowledge of the plans, who had told Bloomberg on Wednesday. The US Treasury could potentially target additional vessels of the Russian shadow fleet that have continued transporting oil, as well as sanctioning traders that have been facilitating in such transactions, according to Bloomberg’s sources, who had added that the sanctions could be announced later this week. The news of additional sanctions follows closely after the latest US sanctions on Russia\'s top oil majors, Rosneft and Lukoil. The effect of these sanctions are still reverberating through the global oil markets and is still upending trade flows: some examples of the effects its had is that Indian buyers are still desperately trying to secure non-sanctioned crude so that its energy security doesn’t fall apart, and Lukoil’s international assets are fighting for US waivers in order to continue normal operations whilst also negotiating asset sales. Kremlin spokesman Dmitry Peskov had commented on the potential new sanctions, stating that “Any sanctions harm the fostering of relations, that is obvious,” reported Russian news agency Interfax. 

 

It has been revealed in the latest survey by the Federal Reserve Bank of Dallas that oil markets continue to suffer with lingering pessimism as oil executives see that there\'s lower activity, on top of higher costs, amid weakening oil prices. Oil executives have predicted that crude will continue to remain dull in 2026, with respondents forecasting that West Texas Intermediate\'s oil prices will bet at $62 per barrel at the end of next year, which is lower than the EIA\'s projections for 2025, at an average of $65.32 per barrel. Statistically, activity in the oil and gas sector had dropped in the final quarter of the year, the business activity index has remained negative at -6.2, which is unchanged from the third quarter. The company outlook index did see a moderate rise from -17.6 to -15.2 in the third quarter of this year, suggesting that negative sentiment is still evident among firms. Moreover, oil and gas production didn’t see much change in the fourth quarter, according to executives at exploration and production firms, adding that the oil production index had experienced a slight rise but kept within the negatives, going from -8.6 in the third quarter to -3.4 in the fourth quarter. Meanwhile, in the third quarter of 2025, the natural gas production index was -3.2, which had jumped to 0 (neutral) in the fourth quarter of this year. Overall, the energy leaders are more bullish about longer-term expectations, but have slightly more optimism for nearer-term. They share the same concerns with many others that if the war between Russia and Ukraine comes to an end, the oil markets could become oversupplied in 2026 since the sanctions on Russian energy will likely be lifted after a peace deal is finalised. However, some positivity shines through as they expect that the markets could become balanced of Iran and Venezuela continues to produce reduced volumes of oil, as well as if the sanctions on Russia remains in place.


The Trump Administration’s trade policies has caused Asia’s energy imports to decline as the reduction of US energy sales in China and India hadn’t managed to offset the increased purchases throughout the rest of Asia. China has significantly cut down on its crude oil and LNG imports from the United States, after no longer purchasing LNG in February due to its trade war with the US, whereas Japan and South Korea had boosted their imports of American crude, according to data from Kpler cited by Reuters columnist Clyde Russell. This year, India had also reduced its LNG imports from America after the two nations had experienced a strong uptake in geopolitical tensions, the increased friction had been caused by the US accusing India that its continued imports of Russian energy commodities is aiding Russia to continue its war with Ukraine. On the other hand, multiple major Asian economies have made trade deals with the United States, negotiations generally involving lower tariffs in exchange for purchasing increased volumes of American energy products. For example, Indonesia  plans to cut down its fuel imports from Singapore and source more refined products from the US - Indonesia\'s state energy firm Pertamina is considering to import oil products from the United States, reported Argus in May. The Southeast Asia’s biggest economy has also implied that it might buy an additional $10 billion worth of American oil and liquefied petroleum gas (LPG). Notably, Japan has purchased 84,500 barrels per day that\'s set to arrive in 2025, compared to only 34,000 barrels per day in 2024, according to Kpler data, meaning that Japan has more than doubled its US crude oil purchases from a year ago. Meanwhile, South Korea had only seen a small increase despite being the top US crude importer in Asia, from 465,000 barrels per day in 2024 to only 470,000 barrels per day this year. Whereas China had just imported 38,350 barrels per day in 2025, cutting its purchases by 84% from 2024, per Kpler data, its significant reduction in US crude purchases has weighed heavily on overall Asian crude imports from America. Kpler data further showed that overall Asian crude imports from the US are set to average at  1.43 million barrels per day this year, which will be down from the average in 2024 of 1.56 million barrels per day, and down from the import record of 1.65 million barrels per day in 2023.

 

The latest data by the Joint Organizations Data Initiative (JODI) had showed on Thursday that a rebound in India\'s product demand has driven a surge in global oil demand during October, compared to September and from October 2024. In October, crude oil exports had jumped by 745,000 barrels per day and oil demand had increased by 677,000 barrels per day from September, meaning that global oil demand had risen by 2.4 million barrels on the tenth month in 2025 compared to last year, according to the most recent data that was self-reported by 48 countries to JODI. India, which is the world’s third largest crude importer after the US and China, had seen its total product demand move up by 201,000 barrels per day in October from a month earlier, revealed JODI data. Additionally, Indian demand had managed to recover seasonally, with people travelling for holiday in October as the June-September monsoon season comes to an end, which usually weighs down on fuel demand particularly as there is low agriculture and construction activity during that season. Whilst India\'s product demand had seen a large influx, China had reported that its total product demand had dropped by 150,000 barrels per day and its crude oil imports had fallen by 28,000 barrels per day in October compared to September. Over the past few months, China\'s demand for road transportation fuels (e.g. gasoline and diesel) has taken some difficult hits due to the surge in registrations of electric passenger cars and LNG-fuelled heavy-duty vehicles, which is the major cause for its recent losses. Private refiner Hengli Petrochemical had commented earlier this month that China\'s oil demand will likely remain in the lower bounds for at least until the middle of 2026. Overall, global crude oil inventories had increased by 36.9 million barrels, while product inventories had declined by 13.9 million barrels in October compared to September. To conclude from these figures, the rise in crude supply, the build-up of onshore inventories, and the increasing rate of oil held in floating storage, has further amplified the ongoing worries of a global glut next year. This prospect has weighed heavily on market sentiment and on oil prices, despite geopolitical events such as the US blockade of sanctioned tankers carrying Venezuelan oil, that would theoretically help ease concerns of an oversupply and would bring some relief to crude oil prices.

China Daily has quoted a new report from the China National Petroleum Corp Economics and Technology Research Institute, which predicts that Chinese crude oil demand will peak by 2030, mainly due to the rate that the nation is transitioning from oil to alternative energy sources. The China National Petroleum Corp Economics and Technology Research Institute has predicted that primary energy demand in China would peak at a level of 5 billion tons by roughly 2035. The forecasters had also said that crude oil will remain a significant part of China\'s energy mix past 2050, oil demand growth for petrochemicals is expected to peak at a level of 290 million tons by 2050, but the mix will look extremely different by 2060. Specifically, electric cars are one of the largest drivers for the downward crude oil demand trend as the change to electric powered transport in China has been progressing faster than previously expected, noted the vice-president of the Economics and Technology Research Institute Wu Mouyuan. However, he did mention that global crude oil demand will remain resilient and will continue growing for longer than previously expected until reaching its peak at a level that’s 5.2% higher the previous forecasts, at around 4.8 billion metric tons, which will likely be supported by developing economies and petrochemicals. Oil demand is expected to peak in 2040 rather than 2030 because of this robust resilience, added Wu.
